# Comprehensive Analysis Report: 8-Channel sEMG & 3D Hand Keypoint Dataset

---

## 1. Concise Summary

This dataset combines 8-channel surface EMG recordings captured via an OpenBCI Cyton biosensing board with 3D hand keypoint data from a LEAP Motion Controller. The primary goal is to analyze the relationship between muscle electrical activity and hand or finger movements in 3D space. Key variables include the eight sEMG channels and the x, y, z coordinates of hand keypoints. Analysis should focus on temporal synchronization of the two data modalities, feature extraction from sEMG signals, and correlation of muscle activations with specific hand gestures or trajectories.

## 2. Dataset Purpose & Context

### What This Dataset Is Fundamentally About

This is a **multimodal biosignal dataset** designed to bridge the gap between *what muscles are doing electrically* and *what the hand is doing physically*. At its core, it is a neuromuscular motion capture dataset — capturing the full chain from muscle activation (sEMG) to physical movement outcome (3D keypoints).

The dataset is classified as a **Time-Series Dataset** (confidence: 0.82), which is the most appropriate archetype given that both sEMG signals and 3D keypoint positions are fundamentally temporal in nature — they evolve continuously over time and derive their meaning from sequential patterns, not isolated observations.

### Instrumentation & Modalities

| Modality | Instrument | Variables |
|---|---|---|
| Surface EMG | OpenBCI Cyton Board | 8 EXG channels (muscle electrical activity) |
| 3D Hand Tracking | LEAP Motion Controller | x, y, z keypoint coordinates |

The **OpenBCI Cyton board** is a research-grade biosensing platform capable of recording high-resolution electrophysiological signals. The **LEAP Motion Controller** provides sub-millimeter 3D hand and finger tracking. Together, these two instruments create a synchronized view of both the *cause* (muscle activation) and the *effect* (hand movement) of voluntary motor actions.

### Primary Variables

The eleven primary variables span two distinct physical domains:
- **sEMG Channels 1–8**: Represent electrical potentials generated by muscle fiber contractions at eight distinct anatomical locations on the forearm or hand.
- **Keypoint X, Y, Z**: Represent the three-dimensional spatial coordinates of specific hand landmarks (joints, fingertips) tracked in real time.

The presence of a **Label** column in the sEMG file further suggests this dataset is structured for **gesture classification** — each labeled segment likely corresponds to a specific hand gesture or movement class.

## 3. Data Relationships & Structure

### The Core Relationship: Muscle Activation → Hand Movement

The fundamental relationship in this dataset is **temporal and causal**: muscle electrical activity (sEMG) precedes and drives the physical hand movements captured as 3D keypoints. This is the electromechanical delay (EMD) principle — neural signals activate muscles milliseconds before observable joint movement occurs.

The dataset captures this relationship across two files:

**File 1 — sEMG Time-Series (54,530 rows × 12 columns):**
This file contains the raw 8-channel EXG recordings with Unix timestamps and formatted datetime values. The `Label` column ties each time-point to a specific gesture class, enabling supervised learning. The sample rows confirm timestamps beginning at `2021-09-15 08:04:11.976`, with samples spaced approximately 3–4 milliseconds apart — consistent with a **~250–333 Hz sampling rate** typical of OpenBCI Cyton configurations.

**File 2 — Combined Feature/Keypoint File (31,594 rows × 27 columns):**
This file appears to represent a fused or processed dataset where sEMG-derived measurements (Measurements 1–8, likely preprocessed channel values) are combined with 3D keypoint coordinates (Measurements 9–27, likely representing x, y, z for multiple hand landmarks). The 27 measurement columns suggest approximately **8 sEMG features + ~19 keypoint coordinates** (e.g., 6–7 keypoints × 3 axes), though the generic column naming (`Measurement_1` through `Measurement_27`) indicates preprocessing has been applied.

### Temporal Synchronization as the Binding Relationship

The two modalities are linked by **timestamp alignment**. The sEMG board and LEAP Motion Controller operate at different native sampling rates, making synchronization a critical preprocessing step. The difference in row counts (54,530 vs. 31,594) between the two files likely reflects this resampling or alignment process — the combined file represents only the temporally overlapping, synchronized portion of the recordings.

## 6. Analysis Recommendations

The following recommendations are drawn directly from the dataset's analytical framework and are appropriate given its **Time-Series, multimodal biosignal** nature:

### Recommendation 1: Temporal Synchronization of Both Modalities
**Why it's appropriate:** The sEMG file contains 54,530 samples while the combined file contains 31,594 rows — a ~42% reduction that almost certainly reflects the temporal overlap between the two recording systems. Since the OpenBCI Cyton and LEAP Motion Controller operate at different native sampling rates, **interpolation or resampling** must be applied to create a unified time axis before any cross-modal analysis. Failure to synchronize will produce spurious correlations or missed relationships between muscle activation and movement.

*Approach:* Use the Unix timestamps in File 1 as the reference timeline. Resample LEAP Motion keypoint data to match the sEMG sampling rate (or vice versa) using linear interpolation, then merge on the common time axis.

### Recommendation 2: Analyze sEMG–Gesture Relationships Across All 8 Channels
**Why it's appropriate:** The dataset purpose explicitly targets the relationship between muscle activation patterns and hand gestures. With 8 channels representing different muscle groups, **multi-channel activation patterns** (not individual channels in isolation) are the meaningful unit of analysis. Different gestures will produce distinct "activation signatures" across the channel array.

*Approach:* Segment the time series by label, then compute per-gesture activation profiles across all 8 channels. Use heatmaps or radar plots to visualize which muscle groups are dominant for each gesture class.

### Recommendation 3: Extract sEMG Features for Gesture Classification
**Why it's appropriate:** Raw sEMG signals are high-frequency, noisy, and not directly interpretable by classification models. Feature extraction transforms the temporal signal into a compact, informative representation. Given the near-zero means and variable standard deviations observed across channels, **amplitude-based features** (RMS, MAV) will capture muscle effort, while **frequency-domain features** (mean frequency, median frequency) will capture fatigue and contraction dynamics.

*Recommended features:*
- **Time-domain**: Root Mean Square (RMS), Mean Absolute Value (MAV), Zero Crossing Rate (ZCR), Waveform Length (WL)
- **Frequency-domain**: Mean Power Frequency (MPF), Median Frequency (MDF) via FFT
- **Window size**: 200–250 ms sliding windows with 50% overlap (standard for gesture recognition)

### Recommendation 4: Visualize 3D Keypoint Trajectories Alongside sEMG Activations
**Why it's appropriate:** The dataset's dual-modality design is best understood visually before building models. Plotting 3D hand trajectories (from keypoint x, y, z coordinates) synchronized with sEMG channel activations over time will reveal **which muscles drive which movements** — for example, whether finger flexion correlates with activation peaks in specific channels.

*Approach:* Create time-aligned dual-panel visualizations: the upper panel showing 8-channel sEMG waveforms, the lower panel showing 3D keypoint trajectories animated over the same time window. This is particularly valuable for validating label accuracy and identifying motion artifacts.

### Additional Consideration: Artifact Rejection
Given the extreme values observed at Sample Index 0 (all channels showing values exceeding -7,000 µV), a **signal quality preprocessing step** should be applied before any analysis. This includes:
- Removing the first N samples affected by electrode settling
- Applying a bandpass filter (typically 20–500 Hz for sEMG)
- Notch filtering at 50/60 Hz to remove power line interference
- Identifying and excluding motion artifact epochs using threshold-based or ICA methods
